The Lego Game

Overview

The Lego Game is a fun way to understand some concepts of Agile and Scrum Development. This game can be conducted with a project team before or after the completion of their project Inception Framework, if their project will follow Agile/Scrum development.

Purpose

Key objectives of this game are to:

  • Understand the history of Agile Development
  • Introduce Scrum techniques
  • Reinforce the importance of the Product Owner
  • Reinforce the importance of communication
  • Introduce the concept of time-bound work durations – Sprints (or iterations)
  • Highlight a few activities that team members are likely to participate in during Sprints
  • Introduce the vocabulary common to iterative development – Story Points, Estimation, Velocity, Hangover, etc.
  • Demonstrate the concept of Adaptive Planning and Evolutionary Development

Players

Led By – One or Two facilitators

Supported By – Business Partners/Product Owners - one for each team

Participation – other members of a project-team or anyone who wants to learn about Iterative/Scrum development.

Initial Planning

In preparation for this game, determine the number of participants for the game and identify the appropriate number of Product Owners (Business Partners). A general rule of thumb is one Product Owner for every four team members. Potential candidates for Product Owners could be anyone who has previously participated in the Lego game, or members of the project team that have previous Agile or Scrum experience and understand the role of the business partner. You will need one box of Legos for every team.

Supplies Needed

The following items will be needed to play the Lego Game:

Legos- one set (including the container) per team

Product Owner instructions

Story cards printed for 3 iterations- one copy per team

Facilitator note-cards

Roadmap Anatomy of XP Game

The requirements are in the form of brief descriptions called User Stories. Each team's Product Owner will give the team a list of stories. The teams have 3 iterations to build what the Product Owner wants.

Estimation: Stories are assigned a number between 1 and 8 indicating the effort required. 1 point indicates Easy, 3-5 points mean the story is medium difficult, 8 points indicate a hard story.

Sign-up: After estimating effort, each team will choose stories that they believe they can complete in an iteration. They will choose stories using a combination of the estimates and business priority.

Develop: Developers start working on the stories. The entire team can work on stories, in any order and with any division of labor they see fit. After completing a piece of work it must be approved by the Product Owner, who will use the acceptance criteria given to them at the start of the game. After a story has been accepted by the Product Owner work can begin on another story on the list.

Test: The Product Owner tests the animal to make sure it meets the acceptance criteria for each story that the team has completed. Bugs are written on cards and added to Backlog.

Retrospective: At the end of development, each team (Product Owner included) will discuss what went well, what didn't go well with the intent of driving toward solutions, and what was confusing or puzzling.
